Central & Southern Aegean

The Central & Southern Aegean region includes some of the most significant biblical and archaeological sites in western Turkey — spanning the Hellenistic, Roman, and Byzantine periods.

Sites include Colossae, Didyma, Ephesus, Euromos, Hierapolis, Knidos, Laodicea, Miletus, Nysa, Priene, Smyrna/Izmir, and more — many of them directly mentioned in the New Testament and Revelation's letters to the Seven Churches.

Colossae

The ancient site of Colossae sits on the southern edge of the Lycus Valley near larger sites such as Laodicea, 8 mi. to the west, and Hierapolis, 13 mi. to the northwest. Paul authored Colossians and Philemon addressed to this city, though he apparently never visited personally.

Didyma

Didyma housed a world-famous oracle of Apollo; the original temple was finished around 560 B.C. before Persian destruction in 494 B.C. A later Hellenistic temple begun around 300 B.C. remained under construction for roughly five centuries.

Ephesus — Upper City

The Upper City is the eastern section of Ephesus, situated in a valley between Mount Pion and Mount Coressus. It served as the political heart of the city and contained multiple temples honoring the Imperial Cult.

Ephesus — Terrace Houses

The Terrace Houses occupy the northern slope of Mount Bülbül. Unit 2 contains 7 residential structures spanning approximately 43,000 square feet, initially built around A.D. 20 and modified until destruction during earthquakes in the third century A.D. Elaborately decorated with tiled floors, frescoes, and marble.

Euromos

Located roughly 44 miles southeast of Ephesus, Euromos contains largely unexcavated ruins from the second century B.C. through second century A.D. Its Temple of Zeus, standing in an olive grove with 16 columns intact, is potentially the finest temple preservation in Turkey.

Hierapolis — Introduction

Hierapolis sits on the northern edge of the Lycus Valley. The Turkish name Pamukkale ('Cotton Castle') comes from the distinctive white travertine cliffs fed by thermal springs. Founded in the early second century B.C. by King Eumenes II of Pergamum, it served primarily as a spa and religious center.

Hierapolis — the Site

Hierapolis sits approximately 12 miles north of Denizli on the Lycus Valley's northern boundary. Founded by Eumenes II of Pergamum, it functioned primarily as a spa and religious hub, gaining renown for producing wool and cloth, particularly purple dye.

According to early Christian tradition, Philip settled at Hierapolis and was martyred here. A fifth-century Martyrium was constructed on a northeastern hill. A 2011 discovery revealed Philip's actual church-tomb approximately 120 feet from the Martyrium.

Knidos (Cnidus)

Knidos was a very important city during the Hellenistic and Roman/Byzantine periods, located on the western tip of the Datça Peninsula. A significant trading hub and home of the Temple of Aphrodite, the city was visited by the Apostle Paul on his voyage to Rome (Acts 27:7).

Laodicea

Laodicea is a very large mound located north of modern Denizli, founded by Seleucid kings during the third century B.C. During the New Testament period it became a major urban center. It is the final church in Revelation's seven-church sequence (Rev. 3:14–22).

Laodicea — Temples

Temple A at Laodicea was established in the first century A.D. and dedicated to Apollo. Over centuries, worship of Artemis was incorporated, followed by Imperial Cult worship added in the early fourth century A.D. during the reign of Diocletian.

Miletus (Balat)

Paul visited Miletus at the end of his third missionary journey (ca. A.D. 57, Acts 20:38), calling together church leaders from Ephesus. Historically a significant seafaring hub that established trading colonies throughout the Mediterranean and Black Sea.

Nysa

Nysa was located on the southern slopes of the Messogis Mountain overlooking the Maeander Valley, founded during the Hellenistic Period in the early third century B.C. Notable remains include a theater, library, and commercial structures.

Priene — General

Priene is an ancient Greco-Roman site 17 miles south of Ephesus, built about 350 B.C. according to the grid plan promoted by Hippodamus of Miletus. Alexander the Great visited in the late fourth century B.C. and contributed financially to the Temple of Athena's construction.

Priene — Religious Buildings

Multiple temples at Priene were dedicated to Athena, Asclepius, Demeter, and Egyptian gods. The Temple of Athena, ordered by Alexander the Great around 333 B.C. and designed by architect Pythius, housed a smaller version of the Parthenon statue.

Smyrna/Izmir

Smyrna (modern Izmir) is located on a deep sheltered bay of the Aegean Sea and was the second of the seven churches addressed in Revelation. Polycarp, burned for his faith at the stadium here, was one of the first Christian martyrs (age 86, A.D. 156).
