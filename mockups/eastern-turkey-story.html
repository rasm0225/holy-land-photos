Eastern Turkey

Eastern Turkey preserves some of humanity's oldest and most dramatic archaeological sites — from the world's earliest known monumental structures at Göbekli Tepe to the mountaintop gods of Nemrut Dagi, the biblical city of Haran, and the great Syro-Hittite kingdoms of the first millennium B.C.

The Euphrates River valley links many of these sites, and the region's museums hold extraordinary mosaics, sculptures, and ancient inscriptions.

Göbekli Tepe

Göbekli Tepe ('Potbelly Hill') is an ancient religious center approximately 9 miles north of Şanlıurfa, active from around 9,600 B.C. to 8,200 B.C. Built by hunter-gatherers — not settled farmers — its limestone T-pillars carved with animals are the oldest known monumental architecture on earth.

Zeugma Mosaic Museum

The Zeugma Mosaic Museum in Gaziantep is the largest mosaic museum in the world, housing artifacts recovered from the ancient city of Zeugma on the western bank of the Euphrates River. The museum opened in 2011 and displays extraordinary floor mosaics from Roman-era villas submerged when the Birecik Dam was completed.

Nemrut Dagi

Nemrut Dagi is a UNESCO World Heritage site in Eastern Turkey, featuring the worship center and burial tumulus of the Commagene king Antiochus I Epiphanes. Standing at 7,050 feet elevation, its colossal stone heads of gods and kings — toppled by earthquakes — are among the most dramatic monuments of the ancient world.

Haran

Haran lies 28 miles south-southeast of Şanlıurfa in southeastern Turkey. The ancient city holds a central place in the biblical narrative as the location where Abraham settled after leaving Ur, and where Isaac and Jacob spent formative years. Its distinctive beehive-shaped mud brick dwellings have been inhabited continuously for millennia.

Sanliurfa

Şanlıurfa — ancient Edessa — is a major city in southeastern Turkey near the Syrian border, venerated as the traditional birthplace of Abraham. Its sacred carp pools (Balıklıgöl) attract pilgrims from across the Islamic world, and its museum houses important finds from the region including artifacts from Göbekli Tepe.

Euphrates River

The Euphrates — one of the four rivers of Eden in biblical geography — flows 1,780 miles through Turkey, Syria, and Iraq before joining the Tigris. In Eastern Turkey the river passes near Carchemish, an ancient crossing point for trade routes between Mesopotamia and the Levant, and through a dramatic mountain landscape before entering the Syrian plain.

08 / 12

Carchemish

Carchemish is a 220-acre site straddling the Turkish-Syrian border on the western bank of the Euphrates, approximately 60 miles northeast of Aleppo. A major Syro-Hittite city-state, it controlled the most important crossing of the upper Euphrates and was the site of the pivotal 605 B.C. battle where Nebuchadnezzar II defeated Pharaoh Neco II — an event documented in both biblical and Babylonian records.

09 / 12

Alalakh — Tell Atchana

Tell Atchana, ancient Alalakh, is a 54-acre mound in Turkey's Amuq Plain occupied from approximately 2200 to 1200 B.C. Once a significant city-state and provincial capital of the Mitanni and Hittite empires, its cuneiform tablets and palace architecture have illuminated the political world of the Late Bronze Age Levant.

10 / 12

Tell Tayinat

Tell Tayinat in the Amuq Plain is the site of ancient Kunulua, capital of the Neo-Hittite kingdom of Patina (Unqi). Excavations have uncovered a tripartite temple with striking affinities to the Solomonic Temple as described in the Bible — one of the most significant architectural parallels known from the ancient Near East.

11 / 12

Zincirli (Sam'al)

Zincirli, ancient Sam'al, is a Neo-Hittite site in Turkey's northern Amuq Plain excavated by the Oriental Institute of the University of Chicago. Its Kulamuwa Inscription — one of the earliest known Phoenician royal inscriptions — and well-preserved city walls make it a key site for understanding the Iron Age kingdoms of the northern Levant.

12 / 12

Yesemek

Yesemek is a Hittite and Neo-Hittite quarry and sculpture workshop covering approximately 25 acres in Eastern Turkey. Reddish-black basalt was quarried here and shaped into rough forms of lions, sphinxes, mountain gods, and other creatures for use at sites across the region. Over 300 unfinished sculptures remain in situ, making it the largest known ancient sculpture workshop in the Near East.
